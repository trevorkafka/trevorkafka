The Kepler Problem

Consider two planets of masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ located at positions $\mathbf r_1$ and $\mathbf r_2$ respectively. The total energy $E$ is the sum of the kinetic energies $\frac{1}{2}m_1 \dot r_1^2$ and $\frac{1}{2}m_2 \dot r_2^2$ assosciated with the movement of the planets and the potential energy $- \frac{G m_1 m_2}{|\mathbf r_1 - \mathbf r_2|}$ assosciated with their gravitational attraction.

\begin{equation} E = \frac{1}{2}m_1 \dot{ \mathbf r}_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 \dot{\mathbf r}_2^2 - \frac{G m_1 m_2}{|\mathbf r_1 - \mathbf r_2|} \end{equation}

By the law of conservation of energy, this quantity $E$ remains constant. By means of a nontrivial identity, this expression can be rewritten through a modification of the kinetic energy portion of the expression as follows.

\begin{equation} E = \frac{1}{2}(m_1+m_2) \left( \frac{m_1\dot{\mathbf r}_1 + m_2 \dot{\mathbf r}_2}{m_1+m_2} \right)^2 + \frac{1}{2}\left( \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2} \right) (\dot{\mathbf r}_1 - \dot{\mathbf r}_2)^2 - \frac{G m_1 m_2}{|\mathbf r_1 - \mathbf r_2|} \end{equation}

Without loss of generality, let us presume that our inertial reference frame from which we observe the planets is such that the velocity of the center of mass of the system is zero: $\frac{m_1\dot{\mathbf r}_1 + m_2 \dot{\mathbf r}_2}{m_1+m_2} = 0$.

\begin{equation} E = \frac{1}{2}\left( \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2} \right) (\dot{\mathbf r}_1 - \dot{\mathbf r}_2)^2 - \frac{G m_1 m_2}{|\mathbf r_1 - \mathbf r_2|} \end{equation}

In the interest of simplicity, define $m = \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2}$, a quantity known as the reduced mass, $\mathbf r = \mathbf r_1 - \mathbf r_2$, the relative position vector, and $\alpha = Gm_1m_2$ in order to simplify as follows.

\begin{equation} E = \frac{1}{2} m \dot{\mathbf r}^2 - \frac{\alpha}{r} \end{equation}

Let's now represent $\mathbf r$ in a polar coordinate system $(r, \theta)$ oriented within the plane of the motion of the planets (the planets are guaranteed to have motion within a plane due to conservation of angular momentum). We can thus write the energy now explicitly via the relationship $\mathbf{\dot r}^2 = \dot{r}^2 + r^2 \dot{\theta}^2$.

\begin{equation} \label{eq:theta-dot} E = \frac{1}{2} m \dot{r}^2 + \frac{1}{2}mr^2 \dot{\theta}^2 - \frac{\alpha}{r} \end{equation}

The corresponding Lagrangian $\mathcal L$ for the system is given as follows.

\begin{equation} \mathcal L = \frac{1}{2} m \dot{r}^2 + \frac{1}{2}mr^2 \dot{\theta}^2 + \frac{\alpha}{r} \end{equation}

The lack of dependence of $\mathcal L$ on $\theta$ implies the conserved angular momentum $L$ is given as follows.

\begin{equation} \label{eq:angular-momentum} L \overset{\text{def}}{=} \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot \theta} = mr^2 \dot \theta \end{equation}

This allows us to introduce this constant $L$ in order to rewrite equation \eqref{eq:theta-dot} as to elimiate $\theta$ in the following manner.

\begin{equation} E = \frac{1}{2} m \dot{r}^2 + \frac{1}{2} \frac{L^2}{mr^2} - \frac{\alpha}{r} \end{equation}

This leaves this relationship expressed only in terms of the coordinate $r$. Solving for $\dot r$ gives the following result.

\begin{equation} \dot r = \sqrt{\frac{2E}{m} - \frac{L^2}{m^2r^2} + \frac{2\alpha}{mr}} \end{equation}

Dividing this result by $\dot \theta = \frac{L}{mr^2}$ from equation \eqref{eq:angular-momentum} yields an expression for $\frac{dr}{d\theta}$.

\begin{equation} \frac{dr}{d\theta} = \frac{\dot r}{\dot \theta} = \frac{\sqrt{\frac{2E}{m} - \frac{L^2}{m^2r^2} + \frac{2\alpha}{mr}}}{\frac{L}{mr^2}} \end{equation}

This is a separable differential equation for the function $r = r(\theta)$. Separating variables yields the following.

\begin{equation} d\theta = \frac{\frac{L}{mr^2}}{\sqrt{\frac{2E}{m} - \frac{L^2}{m^2r^2} + \frac{2\alpha}{mr}}}\ dr \end{equation}

Through some remarkable and nontrivial algebraic rearrangement, this can be reexpressed as follows.

\begin{equation} d\theta = -\frac{-\frac{\frac{L^2}{m\alpha} \frac{1}{r^2}}{\sqrt{1 + \frac{2EL^2}{m\alpha^2}}}}{\sqrt{1 - \left( \frac{\frac{L^2}{m\alpha} \frac{1}{r}-1}{\sqrt{1 + \frac{2EL^2}{m\alpha^2}}} \right)^2}}\ dr \end{equation}

Let $p = \frac{L^2}{m\alpha}$ and $e = \sqrt{1 + \frac{2EL^2}{m\alpha^2}}$.

\begin{equation} d\theta = -\frac{-\frac{p}{er^2}}{\sqrt{1 - \left( \frac{ \frac{p}{r}-1}{e} \right)^2}}\ dr \end{equation}

Integration yields gives the following result.

\begin{equation} \theta = \int -\frac{-\frac{p}{er^2}}{\sqrt{1 - \left( \frac{ \frac{p}{r}-1}{e} \right)^2}}\ dr = \arccos \left( \frac{ \frac{p}{r}-1}{e} \right) + C \end{equation}

Without loss of generality, set $C = 0$ and rearrange for $r = r(\theta)$.

\begin{equation} r(\theta) = \frac{p}{1 + e\cos \theta} \text{ where } \begin{cases} p = \frac{L^2}{m\alpha} \\[0.5em] e = \sqrt{1 + \frac{2EL^2}{m\alpha^2}} \\[0.5em] m = \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1 + m_2} \\[0.5em] \alpha = G m_1 m_2 \\[0.5em] E = \text{energy (constant)} \\[0.5em] L = \text{angular momentum (constant)} \end{cases} \end{equation}

Therefore, the curve swept out by $\mathbf r$ is an ellipse with latus rectum $p$, eccentricity $e$, and one focus at the origin of the polar coordinate system.
