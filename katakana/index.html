Welcome to Katakana Reader, a tool that will allow you to practice and improve your katakana reading abilities.

Tag the katakana in the charts below based on your level of mastery of each by clicking on each katakana. The katakana tagging mode can be switched using the toolbar at the top of the page. Modify your tags as you master reading each katakana. A description of each tag is below.

Tag Description Use Case
(none) These katakana won't appear in words. not-yet-studied katakana
require All tagged katakana appear in each word. Use sparingly. brand-new katakana
emphasize At least one tagged katakana appears in each word. katakana still being learned
allow Other katakana that may be used in any word. Use liberally. well-mastered katakana

After setting your tags, begin to read the words in the word list, which are displayed at random. Click each word to reveal the pronunciation and meaning. The more words you read, the more your reading skills will improve.

Master the katakana from each section before proceeding to the next.

More information
  • Hepburn romanization is used to represent the pronunciation of Japanese words using Latin letters throughout this website, though long vowels are indicated with doubled letters instead of macrons (e.g. - aa instead of ā) to emphasize to learners the function of the vowel extending symbol ー.
  • The list of katakana words, spelling of katakana words, and their English translations are sourced from and August 2025 version of JMdict.

Basic Katakana

Information
  • The katakana below are displayed in traditional alphabetical order, which reflect their phonological categorizations with some exceptions: チ chi, ツ tsu, シ shi, and フ fu.
  • The katakana ヰ wi, ヱ we, and ヲ wo have been omitted because in modern Japanese the combinations ウィ wi, ウェ we, and ウォ wo are used instead (see Vowel Modifiers).
  • In addition to the katakana presented in the main table are three other basic symbols that are common in usage and are recommended to learn sooner rather than later:
    • ン n, which provides solely a nasal consonant sound without a vowel sound,
    • ー (long vowel), which lengthens the sound of the preceeding vowel so that it is pronounced for twice as much time as it would ordinarily, and
    • ッ (glottal stop), which signifies a momentary pause in pronunciation within a word.
    • Note: The katakana ツ tsu and ッ (glottal stop) are distinguished in writing only by their size.

Consonant Modifiers

Information
  • The ゛ dakuten and ゜ handakuten symbols are used in combination with some basic katakana to modify initial consonant sounds mostly according to some regular patterns (k→g, s→z, t→d, h→b→p).
  • The ゜ handakuten symbol is only used in the last row of this table.

Vowel Modifiers

Information
  • The small katakana ャ, ュ, ョ, ァ, ィ, ゥ, ェ, and ォ are used in somewhat consistent ways to modify vowels of existing katakana symbols.
  • These small katakana symbols never appear without a full-size katakana preceeding them and hence do not have sounds on their own. These symbols can be thought of as modifiers on the preceeding full-size katakana.
